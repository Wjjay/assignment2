Global Warming: An Artificial Disaster

Although the idea of economic prosperity sounds nice to us as a species, we tend to ignore some of the side effects. It has been incredibly taxing on the environment as countries continue to industrialize, releasing immense quantities of carbon dioxide into the atmosphere and trapping in solar radiation.

If we continue to ignore the climate crisis, our future will be bleak. On land, we have observed some of the worst bushfires in recent history. In the ocean, coral reefs are dying at an unprecedented rate as increasing water temperatures is causing them to bleach out. This is just the tip of the iceberg.

CO2, the Economy, and Temperatures

We can clearly see that there's a strong positive correlation between CO2 output, GDP per capita, and temperature change.

This does certainly make a lot of sense when we consider economic activities such as mining, manufacturing, and farming consumes tremendous amounts of fossil fuel to operate heavy machinery, transport goods, as well as conducting financial transactions.

CO2 Per Capita by countries

In recent years, Australia's CO2 emission is comparable to economic powerhouses like the United States and China despite having a population size of roughly 20 million people

2010 was the first time when Australia's CO2 emission per capita exceeded that of the United States at 17.581 metric tonnes (vs 17.434 metric tonnes).

Australia has been beating the United States in this department after almost every subsequent year.

So why's Australia such a big pollutor?

It seems like the country heavily relies on fossil fuels to keep up with domestic demands.

Mining eats up a sizeable chunk of the electricity pie. Since 2010, its electricity usage has more than doubled.
Perhaps we can leverage that to reduce our CO2 emissions?

Introducing: Uranium

Uranium is a naturally occurring resource that can be used to generate carbon-neutral electricity. Waste generated by nuclear fission are minimal because according to the World Nuclear Association, around 97% of nuclear by-products are recyclable. Most of the recycled waste will be combined with new nuclear materials to produce additional fuel rods.

Australia has been blessed with almost a third of the world's Uranium deposits. Unfortunately, almost none it have been utilised except for exportation. According to the Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade, the amount of Uranium ores exported from Australia between 2018 and 2019 could generate 97% of Australia's electricity alone.

Despite having a third of the world's supply of Uranium, Australia is only contributing roughly to 10-15% of the world's yearly demands.

Why choose Uranium?

Let's compare various fuels in relation to coal! A kilogram of coal costs 94 US cents at the time of writing, and can produce 0.495 Megajoules of energy while emitting 0.84 kilograms of CO2. We'll use coal as a benchmark for comparing various fuel sources.

If one fuel source has a value of 100 for a particular category, say price, it means that it's as expensive as coal.
If it's 200, then it's 2 times as expensive as coal.

We can see that Uranium outclasses all other popular fuel sources in every category with the exception of price per kg.

However, that doesn't matter because if we consider the amount of energy we get per dollar, Uranium ranks first with 5712 MJ/$USD while other fuels are struggling to hit 200 MJ/$USD. By the way, it's emitting this much energy with 0 CO2 emissions.
